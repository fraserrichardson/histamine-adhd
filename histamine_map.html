§ 01

The system, in one breath.

Histamine is not the villain. It's a signalling molecule the body needs for stomach acid, wakefulness, immune defence and neurotransmission. The trouble starts when production outpaces clearance, which is mostly an enzyme story.

— The clearance pathway —
In
Histamine arrives from food, mast cells, gut bacteria, and is also synthesised endogenously from histidine.
↓
Out 1
DAO (diamine oxidase) degrades histamine in the gut. Made in the intestinal lining. Cofactors: copper, B6, vitamin C.
Out 2
HNMT (histamine N-methyltransferase) clears it inside cells, especially in brain, liver, kidneys. Requires SAMe, so methylation status matters.
↓
Result
If clearance keeps up, you don't notice. If it doesn't, histamine spills over and binds to H1, H2, H3, H4 receptors all over the body, brain included.

The "amine problem" is really a clearance problem. Most people aren't producing pathological amounts of histamine, they've got bottlenecks in the systems meant to take it down.

§ 02

How an amine load actually shows up.

It's a multi-system thing, which is why it gets missed. Same person, same week, can have flushing, brain fog and a 3am wake-up and not connect them.

Symptoms by system
Skin
Flushing, especially cheeks and chest. Itch. Hives. Rosacea flares. Heat intolerance. Reactivity to alcohol, hot showers, exercise.
Brain
Anxiety with no obvious trigger, racing thoughts at night, brain fog, difficulty concentrating, irritability, sensitivity to noise and light.
Sleep
Trouble falling asleep, 2-4am wake-ups (histamine peaks early morning), unrefreshing sleep, vivid dreams when over the threshold.
Gut
Bloating, loose stools or constipation, reflux, food sensitivities that seem to rotate, post-meal headache.
Vascular
Headaches (especially after wine, aged cheese, leftovers), nasal congestion, sneezing, low blood pressure, dizziness on standing.
Cycle
Symptoms worsen around ovulation and pre-menstrually because oestrogen upregulates histamine and downregulates DAO.

The pattern, not any single symptom, is the signal.

§ 03

What feeds the load.

Three taps, one drain. Bring any of these down and the system runs cooler.

Tap 01 — Diet
High-histamine and histamine-liberating foods.
Aged cheese, cured meat, fermented foods (sauerkraut, kombucha, kefir), wine and beer, leftovers more than 24 hours old, smoked fish, tinned fish, vinegar, tomato, spinach, aubergine, avocado, citrus, strawberries, chocolate. Liberators (release stored histamine without containing much) include alcohol, shellfish, additives.
Tap 02 — Gut
Dysbiosis and a leaky barrier.
Certain bacteria (some Lactobacillus strains, Morganella, Klebsiella) produce histamine. Parasites, SIBO and inflammation lower DAO output by damaging the enterocytes that make it. The gut is meant to be the front line of clearance, when it's compromised the load spills systemically.
Tap 03 — Mast cells
Stress, heat, infection, oestrogen.
Mast cells degranulate (release histamine plus tryptase, prostaglandins, cytokines) in response to physical and chemical triggers. Cortisol stabilises them short term but chronic stress sensitises them. Oestrogen primes them, which is why female cycles often track symptoms.
The drain
DAO and HNMT capacity.
Genetics (DAO and HNMT polymorphisms are common), nutrient status (copper, B6, C, SAMe, methyl donors), gut integrity, and interfering substances (alcohol blocks DAO, some medications too) all set how fast the drain empties.
Drain rate > tap rate = no symptoms. Reverse it and you get the picture in §02.
§ 04

The ADHD intersection.

Histamine is itself a neurotransmitter. The brain has its own histaminergic system, projecting from the tuberomammillary nucleus to basically everywhere, and it modulates arousal, attention, and the sleep-wake switch.

Mechanism

H1 and H3 receptors in the cortex and striatum interact with dopamine and noradrenaline, the same systems stimulants act on. H3 is particularly interesting: it's an autoreceptor that, when blocked, increases release of dopamine, noradrenaline and acetylcholine. Pitolisant, a licensed narcolepsy drug, works exactly this way and has been studied in ADHD.

Why this matters for you specifically

  • Stimulants raise noradrenaline, which can also activate mast cells. Some people on stimulants notice flushing, anxiety spikes, or skin reactivity that worsens later in the day.
  • Sleep architecture is histamine-mediated. A high amine load fragments sleep, which then degrades next-day attention and impulse control independent of any ADHD substrate.
  • The "wired but tired" loop: high evening histamine keeps cortical arousal up, you can't switch off, sleep is poor, daytime focus suffers, you compensate with stimulants and stress, mast cells get more reactive. The loop tightens.
  • Anxiety presentations in ADHD often have a histamine component. The phenomenology, racing thoughts plus physical agitation plus heat, looks neurochemical because it is.

This doesn't mean ADHD is "really" histamine. It means there's an upstream modulator that, when noisy, makes every downstream symptom louder. Quieting it gives the dopamine/noradrenaline systems a cleaner signal to work with.

§ 05

The rosacea intersection.

Rosacea is not a textbook histamine disease, but it sits in the same neighbourhood. The shared feature is vascular reactivity in facial skin, and many of the same triggers light up both.

Shared triggers

  • Alcohol, especially red wine
  • Heat: hot drinks, hot showers, sauna
  • Spicy food
  • Stress and emotional arousal
  • Aged and fermented foods
  • Exercise (when over threshold)

Shared mechanisms

  • Mast cell activation in skin
  • Neurogenic inflammation via TRPV1 channels
  • Cathelicidin / LL-37 dysregulation
  • Demodex mite immune response
  • Oxidative stress in dermal vessels
  • Compromised skin barrier

The practical implication: an anti-histamine approach won't cure rosacea, but lowering the systemic amine load takes a real load off the skin's threshold for flaring. People often notice their face is less reactive to things that used to set it off when they bring the rest of the system down.

Sauna deserves a specific note. It's net beneficial for many things you care about (HPA tone, sleep, recovery), but it's a known mast cell activator and a known rosacea trigger. The fix isn't necessarily to stop, it's to time it carefully, hydrate aggressively, and avoid stacking it with other amine loads (alcohol, fermented food, heavy exercise, stress) on the same day.

§ 06

Where NAC fits.

NAC is a useful, slightly underrated tool here, but it doesn't act on histamine directly. It sits one layer up, on the redox and inflammation infrastructure that determines how reactive the whole system is.

— NAC's four-line job description —
01
Glutathione precursor. NAC donates cysteine, the rate-limiting amino acid for glutathione synthesis. Glutathione is the body's main intracellular antioxidant and is needed for phase II liver detox, including clearing aldehydes (a histamine breakdown product).
02
Mast cell stabiliser. Reduces oxidative-stress-driven mast cell degranulation. Less spontaneous release means less histamine into the system in the first place.
03
Mucolytic and biofilm disruptor. Thins mucus, breaks down bacterial biofilms in the gut and sinuses. Helpful when histamine-producing dysbiosis or sinus involvement is part of the picture.
04
Glutamate modulator. Acts on the cystine-glutamate antiporter, which is part of why it has anxiolytic and OCD-spectrum effects. This is independent of histamine but overlaps with the "wired" presentation.

What NAC won't do

It won't directly reduce histamine in food, fix DAO output, or replace antihistamines for acute symptoms. Think of it as raising the floor of the whole system's resilience, not putting out a specific fire.

One nuance worth knowing: NAC can mobilise things during detoxification, and a small minority feel worse before better, especially if methylation is slow. Starting low (600mg) and building is sensible. Take with food.

§ 07

Levers, ranked by leverage.

Not all interventions are equal. These are roughly ordered by impact-to-effort ratio for someone in your situation.

Lever 01 — Highest leverage
Trial a low-histamine diet for 2 to 3 weeks.
This is the diagnostic and the intervention in one. Cut the obvious offenders (alcohol, aged cheese, cured meat, fermented foods, leftovers, vinegar, tomato, citrus, chocolate) and see what changes. If symptoms drop noticeably you have your answer. Then reintroduce one at a time to find your personal threshold. Most people don't need to live restricted long term, they need to know where the ceiling is.

Lever 02
Fix the gut layer.
If DAO is made by gut lining and gut bugs produce histamine, gut work is high-leverage. Your antiparasitic protocol is already doing some of this. After it completes, focus on barrier repair (L-glutamine, zinc carnosine), histamine-friendly probiotics specifically (look for L. plantarum, L. rhamnosus, B. longum, B. infantis, avoid L. casei and L. bulgaricus), and adequate fibre to feed butyrate producers.

Lever 03
Quercetin, the workhorse flavonoid.
Genuine mast cell stabiliser with decent evidence. 500mg twice daily, ideally with bromelain for absorption. This is probably the single most useful targeted addition for both the amine picture and rosacea reactivity. Build for 2 to 4 weeks before judging.

Lever 04
DAO cofactors and direct supplementation.
B6 (P5P form, 25-50mg), vitamin C (500-1000mg), copper (only if not already in your multivitamin, easy to overdo). Direct DAO enzyme supplements (Daosin, Histazyme) are taken 15 min before high-amine meals and can be useful for situational coverage, eating out, travel, social occasions.

Lever 05
NAC as part of your existing stack.
600 to 1200mg daily. You already have this in. The rationale here is the redox and mast cell angle described in §06. Particularly worth keeping during the antiparasitic protocol because biofilm disruption and detoxification load both go up.

Lever 06
Sleep timing and the cortisol curve.
Histamine peaks in the early hours. Anything that pulls cortisol's natural morning rise forward and lets it drop properly in the evening reduces the 2-4am wake. Light in eyes within 30 min of waking, no caffeine after midday, last meal 3 hours before bed, room cool, no alcohol within 4 hours of sleep.

Lever 07
Stress regulation, somatically.
Mast cells are wired into the autonomic nervous system. Sustained sympathetic activation keeps them primed. The TRE, breathwork and bodywork you already do are correctly aimed. Cold exposure has mixed evidence, anti-inflammatory short term, can be a mast cell trigger acutely. Watch your face after.

Lever 08 — Targeted
For rosacea specifically.
Niacinamide topically (4-5%), gentle barrier-supporting routine (no actives, no fragrance), zinc orally if not already covered, omega-3 (you have this), consider azelaic acid or ivermectin cream from a GP if it's persistent. Demodex contribution is worth ruling in or out, treatment is straightforward when it's the driver.

Pull the top three levers properly before adding more. Stacking interventions makes it impossible to know what's working.

§ 08

A working protocol.

A reasonable first pass, given everything you've already got running. Designed to layer cleanly with the antiparasitic schedule and not overload you.

Phased plan
Week 1-2
Diagnostic phase. Drop the obvious high-histamine foods. Add quercetin 500mg twice daily. Keep NAC, omega-3, mag glycinate, B-complex. Note skin, sleep, mood daily, even one line.
Week 3-4
Refine. If clear improvement, start reintroducing foods one at a time, every 3 days. Add B6 (P5P) 25mg if not already covered. Consider DAO enzyme for situational use only.
Post-antiparasitic
Gut repair phase. L-glutamine 5g daily on empty stomach for 4-8 weeks. Histamine-aware probiotic. Reduce restrictive eating gradually as tolerance returns.
Ongoing
Maintenance. Most people land on a sustainable middle ground: aware of triggers, eating broadly but smartly, supplementing cofactors, occasional DAO before known-trigger meals, sleep and stress hygiene non-negotiable.

Things to watch for

  • Symptoms worsening on the protocol can mean the antiparasitic is liberating endotoxin or biofilm contents. Worth pacing rather than abandoning.
  • Rosacea sometimes worsens before improving when overall inflammation is shifting. Two to three week minimum before judging.
  • Stimulant timing can matter more once histamine load drops, you may find you need less, or that the late afternoon rebound smooths out.
  • Oestrogen fluctuations don't apply directly here, but the broader principle does: any systemic inflammatory shift will modulate the picture, including hard training weeks, illness, poor sleep stretches.

This is a working model, not a diagnosis. The point isn't to be right about every mechanism, it's to give you a frame that makes your interventions less random and your observations more legible. Adjust as the evidence comes in.
